\section{Cross-Cutting Trade-offs}

The preceding sections introduced three complementary lenses on embodied manipulation systems: data regimes ($\mathcal{K}_1$--$\mathcal{K}_3$), functional decomposition (B1--B5, P1--P3), and integration architectures (Type~I--III). Each lens is useful in isolation, but the main design tensions of real systems only become visible when these axes are considered jointly. This section therefore shifts from categorization to comparison. Rather than asking how systems are labeled, it asks what they gain, what they sacrifice, and under which constraints those trade-offs become decisive. Across recent embodied manipulation systems, four cross-cutting tensions appear repeatedly: semantic breadth versus control precision, architectural unification versus execution responsiveness, data scale versus embodiment specificity, and benchmark performance versus deployment readiness.

\subsection{Semantic Breadth versus Control Precision}

A recurring pattern in recent systems is the tension between broad semantic competence and precise physical execution. Architectures that absorb perception, reasoning, and action generation into a highly unified backbone often benefit from large-scale pretraining, open-vocabulary conditioning, and flexible task specification. These properties make them attractive for instruction following, semantic generalization, and broad task coverage. However, the same architectural unification can weaken explicit handling of geometry, contact structure, and control timing. In manipulation, success often depends not only on understanding what object is being referred to, but on maintaining stable contact, respecting spatial tolerances, and reacting to sub-second execution errors. Systems that excel at semantic breadth therefore do not automatically achieve robust physical precision.

This trade-off is one reason the field has not converged on a single dominant architecture. Type~I systems often maximize semantic coverage by allowing language, vision, and action to share a common representational pathway, but they can struggle when the task requires explicit geometric grounding or low-latency corrective control. Type~III systems make the opposite commitment: by maintaining an explicit state representation, they often improve geometric consistency, pre-execution verification, and contact-aware planning, but may inherit brittleness from imperfect state estimation or predictive drift. Type~II systems occupy an intermediate position, using hierarchy to preserve some semantic flexibility while isolating fast execution from slower deliberation. The central point is that semantic intelligence and manipulation reliability are not interchangeable achievements; different architectures privilege them differently.

\subsection{Architectural Unification versus Execution Responsiveness}

A second trade-off concerns how tightly planning and action are integrated. End-to-end unification reduces interface engineering and can simplify training objectives, but it also concentrates multiple functions into a single computational pathway. In real robotic control, this can create tension with responsiveness. Manipulation requires decisions under strict control-loop constraints, especially in contact-rich settings where delayed correction can convert a recoverable deviation into outright failure. Systems that rely on autoregressive inference or deep deliberative passes may therefore achieve strong offline capability while remaining difficult to execute at stable real-time frequencies.

Hierarchical and state-mediated systems address this problem differently. Type~II architectures separate slower high-level reasoning from faster low-level execution, making it possible to preserve semantic or strategic planning while keeping control loops lightweight. This often improves runtime responsiveness, but it transfers difficulty to the interface between levels: high-level outputs must be expressed in a form that low-level controllers can interpret robustly and consistently. Type~III architectures instead place an explicit world representation between reasoning and action, enabling planning to operate over persistent structure rather than raw observations alone. This can improve verification and physical grounding, but the system now depends on maintaining state fidelity under partial observability, distribution shift, and temporal drift. In practice, architectural unification simplifies system structure, whereas execution-oriented decomposition simplifies control stability; neither dominates universally.

\subsection{Data Scale versus Embodiment Specificity}

The third trade-off concerns what kind of data best supports generalizable manipulation. Larger datasets increase coverage, but not all kinds of diversity contribute equally to embodied competence. $\mathcal{K}_1$ datasets provide dense supervision over local interaction phenomena and are often most useful for learning reliable primitives such as grasping, insertion, and contact-sensitive adjustment. Their strength lies in physical grounding, but their weakness is that they usually under-specify long-horizon task composition. $\mathcal{K}_2$ datasets add temporal structure and task-level supervision, making them better suited for instruction-conditioned behavior and multi-step task completion. Yet these datasets are often tied to a fixed embodiment family, meaning that compositional competence does not automatically transfer across robots. $\mathcal{K}_3$ datasets attempt to address this by broadening supervision across embodiments, sensors, and collection pipelines, but doing so introduces new alignment problems: action spaces differ, observation interfaces are heterogeneous, and standardization is partial at best.

As a result, more data is not a sufficient description of progress. What matters is how data scale interacts with embodiment specificity and learning objective. Primitive-rich data can outperform broader corpora when physical precision is the dominant bottleneck; task-scale data can improve instruction following without solving transfer; and cross-embodiment datasets can expand generalization while weakening consistency of supervision. This is why different data regimes tend to favor different architectural commitments. Systems trained primarily on $\mathcal{K}_1$ often benefit from architectures that preserve contact sensitivity and control structure. Systems centered on $\mathcal{K}_2$ can exploit stronger semantic planning and policy composition. Systems that aim to leverage $\mathcal{K}_3$ must cope not only with scale, but with heterogeneity in embodiment and interface. The key trade-off is therefore not simply between small and large datasets, but between physically specific supervision and transferable but less uniform experience.

\subsection{Benchmark Performance versus Deployment Readiness}

The final and perhaps most consequential trade-off is the gap between benchmark success and deployment readiness. Benchmarks reward many valuable properties, including task completion, generalization across held-out objects, and instruction following. However, they often under-measure properties that dominate real-world deployment: latency stability, recovery from unexpected contact events, robustness to calibration drift, sensitivity to sensing failures, and safe behavior under distribution shift. A model may therefore appear strong under standard evaluation while remaining fragile in long-horizon or safety-critical execution.

This gap becomes clearer when viewed through the three deployment stages introduced in the following section. Stage~1 improvements such as compression, distillation, or sim-to-real adaptation may raise offline efficiency or transferability, but they can also constrain later adaptation capacity. Stage~2 alignment on real hardware can significantly improve target-domain performance, yet may introduce weight changes that complicate Stage~3 execution, especially under quantization or runtime memory constraints. Stage~3 finally exposes whether earlier design choices survive the realities of inference-time control: bounded latency, finite compute, failure recovery, and anomaly detection. For this reason, benchmark gains should be interpreted as only one component of system quality. A system is deployment-ready not when it performs well on held-out tasks alone, but when its semantics, control pathway, embodiment assumptions, and runtime constraints remain compatible under closed-loop physical execution.

Taken together, these trade-offs explain why embodied manipulation has not converged on a single scaling law or architectural recipe. Different systems optimize different interfaces: between semantics and control, between unification and responsiveness, between data breadth and embodiment specificity, and between benchmark capability and deployable robustness. The purpose of the preceding taxonomies is therefore not to rank all methods under one hierarchy, but to clarify which compromises each regime makes and why those compromises matter in practice.
