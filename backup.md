\section{Open Challenges and Future Directions}\label{sec:challenges}

The preceding sections reviewed embodied manipulation across hardware, data, learning, deployment, and evaluation. Taken together, they suggest that the central open problems are no longer confined to any single module. Instead, they increasingly arise from how embodiment, data, model design, deployment constraints, and evaluation protocols interact with one another. Hardware limits what can be sensed and collected; data regimes influence which learning strategies are viable; architectural choices affect deployability; and evaluation protocols shape which capabilities the field learns to prioritize. In this sense, the remaining challenges are distributed across the stack, but they are most acute at its interfaces. We therefore group them into six categories, following the overall pipeline while emphasizing the cross-stage dependencies that now dominate progress.

\subsection{Embodiment}\label{sec:challenge_embodiment}

Current manipulation systems still operate within a narrow sensory and control envelope. Most large-scale pipelines remain centered on RGB or RGB-D perception, sometimes augmented with wrist-mounted force-torque sensing, while tactile sensing at the fingertip or palm level remains rare in both datasets and deployed policies. This imbalance is not merely a matter of missing modalities; it directly limits the class of tasks that learned systems can perform robustly. Contact-rich insertion, deformable object handling, in-hand reorientation, and failure recovery all depend on signals that vision alone cannot provide reliably, especially under occlusion or rapid contact transitions.

Embodiment diversity introduces a second challenge. Real robots differ substantially in end-effector morphology, kinematic structure, joint limits, sensing layout, compliance properties, and low-level control interfaces. These differences propagate throughout the learning stack. Data collected on one robot often transfers poorly to another without embodiment-specific retargeting; action spaces and control frequencies must be normalized manually; and seemingly similar policies can behave very differently when deployed on distinct hardware. Existing cross-embodiment datasets broaden platform coverage, but they do not eliminate the underlying mismatch between platforms.

The deeper question is therefore not only how to support more embodiments, but how embodiment should enter the learning problem itself. One possibility is that the field will converge toward a smaller number of reference platforms, reducing variation through de facto standardization. Another is that policy architectures will need stronger abstraction layers that explicitly decouple task intent from platform-specific actuation. A third possibility is tighter co-design between learned policies and the downstream control stack, so that the policy is aware of the servo loops, compliance controllers, and safety monitors that mediate its actions. At present, none of these directions has clearly emerged as dominant, which leaves embodiment not as a solved substrate, but as an active source of uncertainty for the entire pipeline.

\subsection{Data Acquisition and Reuse}\label{sec:challenge_data}

The central data bottleneck is no longer raw collection volume alone, but the difficulty of making heterogeneous data reusable across robots, tasks, and operating conditions. Teleoperation remains expensive and operator-dependent; autonomous exploration can scale collection but often lacks task diversity and meaningful long-horizon structure; and human demonstration, while abundant in principle, introduces embodiment mismatch that must later be corrected. The resulting corpora differ widely in sensing modalities, control interfaces, action parameterizations, annotation conventions, and embodiment assumptions, making cross-dataset reuse difficult without substantial bespoke alignment.

Simulation and synthetic generation can reduce collection cost, but they do not resolve the problem cleanly. Physics engines still struggle with contact, friction, compliance, and deformable dynamics, while procedurally generated scenes or learned world models raise a different question: whether synthetic trajectories are not only diverse, but physically meaningful and useful for downstream policy learning. The difficulty is especially acute for the data regimes that matter most in real deployment, including failures, recoveries, contact-rich transitions, and cross-embodiment adaptation. These are precisely the trajectories that are most informative and also the hardest to collect or synthesize at scale.

A further uncertainty concerns scaling behavior itself. It remains unclear whether embodied manipulation data obeys scaling laws similar to those observed in language and vision, or whether returns saturate earlier because every trajectory is grounded in specific physics, embodiment, and task structure. If manipulation data scales differently, then simply collecting more trajectories may be less effective than improving diversity, mixture quality, and reuse efficiency. The data problem is therefore becoming increasingly structural rather than merely volumetric. What the field lacks is not only more data, but data infrastructures that make heterogeneous supervision interoperable enough to be reused across settings without extensive re-engineering at every new platform boundary.

\subsection{Learning: Policy, Planning, and Integration}\label{sec:challenge_learning}

At the learning level, progress has revealed a broad design space without yet producing strong selection principles. Different policy families continue to expose different structural trade-offs. Explicit continuous policies remain attractive for their simplicity and low inference cost, but they are prone to mode averaging when demonstrations contain multiple valid responses. Discrete-action policies capture multimodality more naturally, yet pay for this with quantization error and sequential latency. Iterative generative policies offer the richest distributional expressiveness, but their inference cost, sensitivity to distribution shift, and deployment complexity remain significant obstacles, especially in contact-rich or reactive settings.

Planning is similarly unsettled. Semantic and logical planners offer abstraction and modularity, but they usually rely on external motion layers whose feasibility assumptions are not verified until execution. Grounded and geometric planners bring reasoning closer to the physical scene, yet still face a substantial gap between what is represented and what is actually executable. Predictive planners learn forward dynamics or future state distributions, but accumulated error over long horizons continues to limit their reliability. As a result, no single planning regime has yet emerged as a stable solution across long-horizon reasoning, geometric grounding, and physical feasibility.

These tensions become even sharper at the integration level. Type~I systems absorb perception, reasoning, and action generation into a single forward path, maximizing representational sharing but making execution sensitive to the mismatch between semantic reasoning timescales and motor control timescales. Type~II systems separate slow deliberation from fast execution, but turn the intermediate interface itself into a major design variable whose representation may be linguistic, latent, visual, symbolic, or action-like, without a clear rule for when each is preferable. Type~III systems maintain explicit world state, but must keep that state both geometrically accurate and temporally consistent under partial observability and long-horizon evolution. The unresolved issue is therefore not the absence of architectural options, but the lack of principled criteria for choosing among them. A mature theory of embodied learning would need to map task requirements, embodiment complexity, data regime, and control budget to appropriate planning, policy, and integration structures, rather than leaving these choices empirical and retrospective.

\subsection{Deployment}\label{sec:challenge_deployment}

Deployment introduces a second layer of incompatibility across stages. Pre-deployment preparation emphasizes transferability, compression, and sim-to-real alignment; online alignment emphasizes adaptation under real friction, sensor noise, and out-of-distribution conditions; inference-time execution emphasizes low latency, closed-loop responsiveness, and efficient edge deployment. In practice, these objectives do not compose cleanly. A model that is compression-friendly before deployment may be destabilized by aggressive adaptation; a model that adapts well online may become harder to quantize or serve efficiently; and a model that runs efficiently at inference time may sacrifice precisely those action-sensitive dimensions that matter most for fine manipulation.

This means that deployment cannot be understood as a simple final stage appended after training. It is better viewed as a sequence of tightly coupled transformations in which earlier design decisions constrain later ones, and later deployment needs feed back into what should have been optimized upstream. Sim-to-real grounding may fail because the assumptions embedded in data and representation do not survive physical contact. Online adaptation may improve local performance while undermining structure needed for efficient execution. Quantization and acceleration may recover speed at the cost of action fidelity or robustness. Current methods still tend to optimize these stages separately, even though the practical system must satisfy them jointly.

The broader challenge is therefore to make preparation, adaptation, and execution mutually compatible. What is needed is not simply better fine-tuning, better compression, or better serving in isolation, but deployment pipelines whose stages preserve rather than undermine one another. Until such pipelines emerge, deployment will remain a source of nonlinear failure modes rather than a predictable continuation of training.

\subsection{Evaluation}\label{sec:challenge_evaluation}

Evaluation remains limited in both scope and standardization. Existing metrics have become more fine-grained for execution quality, data efficiency, and transfer validity, but they are still concentrated around a relatively narrow set of task formats and embodiments. Many execution metrics remain tuned to single-arm tabletop manipulation; many sample-efficiency metrics stop at learning outcome without saying much about robustness; and many sim-to-real proxies remain weakly calibrated under embodiment shift, contact-rich dynamics, and long-horizon execution. As a result, what is measured is often only a partial proxy for what matters in deployment.

A more subtle issue is that benchmarks do not merely reflect progress; they also shape it. Once a small set of suites becomes dominant, research naturally adapts to their task distributions, observation formats, and success criteria. This can bias architectural development toward what is easy to compare, rather than what is broadly needed for embodied manipulation. Approaches that depend on different interfaces, richer intermediate states, or more complex deployment assumptions often become difficult to evaluate fairly, not because they are less relevant, but because benchmark formats do not accommodate them.

Real-world evaluation introduces a separate reproducibility problem. Results collected in different laboratories are rarely directly comparable, even when task descriptions appear similar, because embodiment, calibration, sensor placement, scene layout, intervention policy, and environmental conditions differ substantially. Without stronger shared physical protocols, real-world validation remains fragmented. The field therefore lacks not only broader metrics, but evaluation frameworks that can jointly account for execution quality, transfer, deployment cost, and reproducible real-world testing. In this sense, evaluation is not simply lagging behind progress; it is becoming one of the main bottlenecks on progress itself.

\subsection{Safety, Interpretability, and Broader Concerns}\label{sec:challenge_safety}

Safety spans the entire embodied stack rather than any single component. Learned policies remain vulnerable to adversarial perturbations, poisoned data, and hidden failure modes introduced during training or deployment. Generative and chunked action policies can introduce systematic reaction delays when the environment changes during execution. At the control interface, low-level safety constraints are often enforced only after a policy has produced its output, with limited guarantees that policy behavior and safety monitors are jointly compatible. This leaves a gap between nominal policy competence and trustworthy deployment.

Interpretability compounds the safety problem. Current VLA, diffusion, and world-model-based systems expose limited visibility into how perception is translated into action. When failures occur, it is often unclear whether the problem lies in observation encoding, reasoning, action representation, or interface mismatch with downstream controllers. Hierarchical and chain-of-thought-style systems provide more visible intermediates, but these intermediates are not guaranteed to faithfully reflect the true basis of the final decision. Without more reliable interpretability, it remains difficult to diagnose errors, predict failure conditions, or certify systems for operation in human-shared environments.

These technical concerns connect directly to broader deployment questions. Human oversight is still weakly calibrated: many systems oscillate between full autonomy and rigid intervention triggers, with limited ability to judge when help is genuinely needed. Trust depends not only on success rate, but on predictable failure modes, interpretable behavior, and graceful degradation. Beyond this, embodied AI also raises concerns about training and deployment cost, labor substitution, data ownership, liability, and governance in shared physical spaces. These questions extend beyond technical system design, but they will increasingly shape which embodied systems can be built, deployed, and socially accepted.

Taken together, these challenges suggest that the next phase of progress in embodied manipulation will depend less on pushing isolated modules further and more on closing the loop between them. Better hardware without reusable data, larger datasets without principled architectures, stronger models without deployable interfaces, and better benchmarks without real-world validity will each produce only partial gains. What remains most needed is system closure: interfaces that are explicit, measurable, and revisable across embodiment, data, learning, deployment, and evaluation. Without that closure, progress will continue to appear faster in isolated benchmarks than in sustained embodied operation.
